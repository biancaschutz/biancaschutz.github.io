National Park Adventures

August 30, 2025

In 2025, I’ve been to nine national parks, and each of them has been a life-changing experience. In January, we snowshoed in Glacier NP while skiing in Whitefish, MT. In March, my friends and I drove up from LA to Yosemite National Park. In July and August, I spent over three weeks driving around the Rocky Mountains from Austin, Texas all the way up to Yellowstone in Wyoming. Here’s my recap, through data and photos!

Why National Parks?

When I was younger, going on a hike was my absolute nightmare weekend activity. Growing up going to New Mexico, I would avoid hiking at all costs. If I was forced to go, I would either wait at the trailhead or stick earbuds in and get it over as quickly as possible.

This all changed in July 2023, hiking in the Swiss Alps. Walking up into the mountains, by waterfalls, huts, and wildlife, I felt at peace listening to the sounds of the birds, the cowbells, the flowing water, and the wind. All of a sudden, hiking wasn’t so bad.

I started thinking of my summer graduation trip that year, and continued to plan it into 2024 and nail down the specifics in 2025. Meanwhile, we went to Glacier in January. As I planned, the Trump administration ripped away staffing and funding from the parks. I felt determined to continue supporting our parks as the government continued to turn their backs on these lands. After seeing these parks, I’ve seen natural beauty unlike any other and places that feel like true magic. These lands must be protected for generations to come to find the same happiness and inspiration I found in them.

2025 Recap

My Hiking Stats

While hiking in national parks and forests this year, I totaled more than 70 miles and 17,000 feet of elevation gain. The longest hike was in a National Forest in Colorado, which also had the most elevation gain. We hiked up to over 12,500 feet to see lakes at the base of several 14ers, it was absolutely stunning.

We did the most hiking in Yellowstone - almost 18 miles - followed by Zion, RMNP, and GTNP. Interestingly, this seems to be correlated with which parks were my favorites, though Yosemite is an outlier with that trend since we visited in winter where more trails are closed/covered in snow.

Total Mileage and Elevation By Park

Aside from these tracked activities, we walked a lot. I broke 30,000 steps twice over the course of the trip, and only had less than 10,000 steps on days we were driving (such as from Wyoming to Colorado) and three other days that were partial drive days.

Impact of Funding and Staffing Cuts

While some parks have managed to overcome the 24% staffing cuts since April by reassigning staff priorities, others were still struggling by July with a variety of closures and service reductions. The New York Times studied the impacts of the cuts on Zion National Park specifically, where we saw the fee entrance station reduced hours and facility closures first-hand.

Based on an internal report obtained from the National Parks Conservation Association, I found that 19 of the 63 national parks are currently experiencing issues caused by staffing shortages. The map below shows parks by color based on whether they currently are suffering the effects of the cuts. Users can filter to view only parks experiencing issues, search for a particular park, and click on the park to see what issues they’re experiencing.

Issues Reported By Park

Arches, Canyonlands, Capitol Reef, Mount Rainier, Sequoia & Kings Canyon, Wind Cave, and Yosemite all reported issues in April but have resolved the issues by onboarding seasonal staff or reassigning staff to address the closures.

The internal report categorized the closures and reduced services by type, which I mostly adhered to with those used in this analysis, but I also reported additional closures based on the parks’ descriptions of the issues. For example, Death Valley has experienced delays in disaster relief projects, which the report marked as “Roads,” but I also marked that Death Valley’s disaster relief has been affected by staffing shortages, as described in the report. “Entrances” refers to any affected fee collection sites, contact stations, or park entrances. “Facilities” refers to closures related to bathrooms and water services. “Programs” refers to any Ranger-led tours, educational programs, and permits.

What Can We Do?

These parks are under attack by our government, who would rather strip these parks bare for their natural resources. Public lands are crucial to biodiversity and our environment, sacred to Indigenous communities, and important for so many other reasons.

Whether you’ve visited a national park, monument, or historical site, intend to, or just happen to read this, these public lands need our support. Call your representatives, visit responsibly, donate, volunteer! Keep these lands beautiful, sacred, and enjoyable for generations to come.
